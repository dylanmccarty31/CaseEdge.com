Leadership Core

Tell me about a time you had to influence someone without formal authority.

‚Äî
STAR Framework Guidance
Situation: Set the scene ‚Äî who was involved, what was at stake, why alignment mattered.
Task: Explain your role and the specific challenge of influencing without authority.
Action: Focus on how you built credibility, found common ground, or used data/logic to persuade. This is the most important part.
Result: Quantify the outcome. What changed? What was the business impact?
Sample Good Answer
During my internship at a regional bank, I identified that our loan processing team and the compliance team had misaligned workflows causing a 3-day average delay in approvals. I had no authority over either team, but I organized a joint working session where I presented data showing the delay was costing the bank roughly $180K in deferred revenue per quarter. I proposed a shared checklist and a weekly 20-minute sync. Both team leads agreed to pilot it for 30 days. By the end of the pilot, average processing time fell from 7 days to 4 days ‚Äî a 43% improvement ‚Äî and both teams asked to make it permanent.

Failure & Growth Core

Describe a significant failure. What happened and what did you learn?

‚Äî
STAR Framework Guidance
Key Insight: Interviewers are testing self-awareness and growth mindset, not competence. Pick a real failure that had genuine stakes ‚Äî not something trivial.

Situation/Task: Briefly explain the context and your responsibility.
Action: Own the failure clearly ‚Äî don't deflect or minimize. What decisions or actions were yours?
Result + Learning: Explain what changed in you. Can you show evidence of the learning in a later situation?
Sample Good Answer
In my junior year, I led a team case competition and we placed last. Looking back, I had micromanaged every section instead of trusting my teammates' expertise. I assumed I needed to control all outputs to ensure quality, which actually slowed us down and discouraged the team. After the competition, I had honest conversations with each teammate ‚Äî and the feedback confirmed it. In my next team project, I delegated ownership of sections with clear milestones and only reviewed final drafts. We won regionals. The core lesson was that trust and delegation aren't a risk to quality ‚Äî they're a precondition for it.

A great version of this answer includes explicit evidence that you've applied the lesson under higher stakes ‚Äî ideally in a professional context post-internship...

Conflict

Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager or a senior stakeholder. How did you handle it?

‚Äî
STAR Framework Guidance
What they're testing: Professional maturity, courage, and judgment. Pick a substantive disagreement on a real issue ‚Äî not a style preference.

Action: Show that you raised your concern respectfully but directly, backed it with data or logic, and ultimately deferred once you'd been heard ‚Äî unless the stakes were ethical.
Result: Ideally show the disagreement led to a better outcome, even if you eventually followed the manager's call.
Sample Good Answer
During a summer internship at a consulting firm, my project manager wanted to include a market sizing assumption in our final deck that I believed was too aggressive by roughly 40%. I ran a second analysis using three different approaches ‚Äî top-down, bottom-up, and comparable market benchmarks ‚Äî and all three pointed to a lower figure. I requested a 15-minute conversation, walked her through my methodology, and shared the models. She reviewed them, agreed the assumption needed to be revised, and we updated the deck. The partner review actually highlighted our rigor on this point as a strength of the analysis. The experience taught me that well-supported pushback, delivered respectfully, is almost always welcomed at good firms.

Teamwork

Give me an example of a time you had to work with a difficult colleague or team member.

‚Äî
STAR Framework Guidance
Trap to avoid: Don't make the colleague sound like a villain. Interviewers are evaluating your empathy and professionalism, not your ability to complain.

Action: Show that you tried to understand the person's perspective first. What did you do to address the root cause ‚Äî not just the symptom?
Result: Ideally show that the relationship improved and the work succeeded.
Sample Good Answer
On a group project in my MBA program, one team member consistently missed deadlines and was uncommunicative. Rather than escalate immediately, I asked for a private conversation to understand what was going on. He shared that he was dealing with a family health situation he hadn't wanted to mention. We restructured his workload to focus on tasks he could complete asynchronously, redistributed the time-sensitive pieces, and built in a check-in system. He was able to contribute meaningfully, the project was submitted on time, and our team received one of the highest grades in the class. I learned that performance issues often have underlying causes ‚Äî curiosity is more effective than frustration.

Achievement Core

Tell me about your greatest professional or academic achievement. What made it significant?

‚Äî
STAR Framework Guidance
Choose wisely: Pick something with scope, complexity, or ambition ‚Äî not just a good grade. Ideal if it shows initiative beyond what was required.

Action: Walk through specifically what YOU did ‚Äî not the team. Be specific about your contributions.
Result: Quantify wherever possible. Significance = scale of impact or difficulty of accomplishment.
Sample Good Answer
During my senior year, I launched a student investment fund from scratch ‚Äî no blueprint, no existing funding, no faculty sponsor initially. I recruited 14 members, wrote our investment charter, secured $50,000 in seed capital from alumni, and built our risk and reporting infrastructure over four months. In our first year, the fund returned 14.3% against an 8.1% benchmark. More importantly, 9 of the 14 members went on to finance roles at bulge-bracket banks or asset managers, and they cited the fund directly in their recruiting conversations. I'm most proud of it because the challenge was mostly organizational and persuasion-based ‚Äî I had to make something exist that didn't before.

A great answer draws on your specific background and career trajectory to make the achievement feel inevitable and authentic rather than rehearsed...

Strategy & Goals

Why consulting / why this firm? What do you bring that others don't?

‚Äî
How to Think About This
This is a "Why us + Why me" question. Answer both parts directly.

Why consulting: Tie it to a specific experience that revealed your interest in problem-solving at scale across industries. Be specific ‚Äî not generic.
Why this firm: Reference specific practices, culture, or work. Show you've done research.
What you bring: Name 1‚Äì2 concrete differentiators tied to evidence.
Sample Good Answer
I became serious about consulting after leading a supply chain redesign project for a small manufacturing firm during my MBA. For the first time I was working across strategy, operations, and finance simultaneously ‚Äî and I found that synthesis more energizing than any functional role. I want to do that across many industries, and at scale. I'm targeting Bain specifically because of how they approach private equity advisory ‚Äî particularly the work done through the MACS practice. I've spoken with three Bain consultants who all described the same culture: strong accountability, collaborative rather than hierarchical, and intellectually honest. What I bring distinctively is a combination of quantitative training from my engineering background and real operating experience ‚Äî I've actually implemented the kinds of recommendations I'd be helping clients develop.
